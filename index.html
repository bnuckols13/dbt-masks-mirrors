DBT Skills — Adolescent Group (Virtual)

Masks & Mirrors

Exploring the space between who you show and who you are

Welcome

5 to 8 minAbout today

Today's group is an art therapy session on Zoom. You'll choose from seven creative stations, each offering a different way to explore the same question: what's the difference between the version of you that other people see and the version of you that exists when no one is watching?

There is no right way to do this. You don't need to be an artist. You don't need to share anything you make. Use whatever materials you have at home. Camera on or off is your choice at every station.

Confidentiality

What's shared in group stays in group. If you reference group outside this room, talk about your own experience only. Other people's stories are not yours to tell.

Participation

You can pass at any point. No explanation needed. If something doesn't fit right now, say "pass" and we move on.

Opening: Hands as Mirrors

Find a small object near you: a stone, a pen, a coin, anything that fits in one hand. This is your grounding object for the session.

Three slow breaths. On each exhale, let your hands rest open in your lap, palms up. Notice the temperature, weight, and texture of the stone against your skin.

"Close your hands around the stone. Notice what it feels like to hold something hidden, something only you can feel. You don't have to name it."

"Now open your hands again. Notice the difference. Not better or worse. Just different."

This is a mindfulness observe-and-describe exercise. You're practicing noticing physical sensation without judging it.

Check-in

8 to 10 minBridge questions

Answer whichever one pulls you. One or two sentences is enough.

1
When was the last time you felt like someone saw the real you?
Could be a specific moment, a relationship, or a context where you felt less filtered.
2
What's one thing your "public self" does that your private self finds exhausting?
Think about performances you maintain at school, online, or at home. What costs you energy?
3
If you could take off one mask for the rest of today, which one would it be?
Name the mask by what it does ("the one that says I'm fine," "the one that smiles when I'm angry").
Understanding

10 minMasks, mirrors, and the space between

Everyone curates how they appear. On social media, at school, in families, in treatment. This is normal. The question isn't whether you do it, but whether you get to choose when.

What masks are +

A mask is any version of yourself you present because you believe the real version won't be accepted, safe, or good enough. Masks aren't inherently bad. Some protect you in genuinely unsafe situations. The problem starts when you can't tell the difference between choosing a mask and being trapped inside one.

In DBT terms, masks relate to interpersonal effectiveness. The FAST skill (Fair, no Apologies, Stick to values, Truthful) describes what it looks like to show up without a mask in relationships. Most people find this terrifying.

Why we wear them +

Masks serve real functions. They manage other people's perceptions. They prevent rejection. They keep the peace. For many people in recovery, the eating disorder itself functions as a mask: a way to communicate distress without having to say it directly, or a way to feel in control of how the world perceives you.

Recognizing why you wear a mask is not the same as taking it off. Today's group is about noticing the gap between inside and outside. What you do with that information is your choice, on your timeline.

What mirrors reveal +

A physical mirror shows the outside. Art can show the inside. The stations in this group offer seven different "mirrors" for parts of yourself that don't have a reflection yet.

In DBT, this connects to mindfulness: the practice of observing your internal experience without judging it or trying to change it. And to validation: treating your own feelings and experiences as real and understandable, even the ones you hide.

The word "mirror" in this session is always metaphorical. There are no physical mirrors in the room. The point is to see yourself through what you create, not through how you appear.

Coming back together

8 to 10 minGallery share

If you're willing, hold your work up to the camera or screen-share a photo. You can also describe what you made without showing it. Anonymous descriptions are fine: "Someone made a piece that..." works too.

As each person shares, type one word in the chat that captures your response. No verbal commentary during the share. Just the word.

Circle

5 to 7 minOne true thing

Each person completes one sentence:

"One true thing I noticed today is _______________."

This can reference your own work, someone else's, a feeling you had, or something about the process. After each person shares, the group response is one word: "Heard." No cross-talk, no follow-up questions.

This week

3 minPick one

Choose one thing to try before next group. You're not committing to a habit. You're running a single experiment.

Notice one mask
Catch yourself performing a version of you that doesn't match what you feel. Just notice. Don't change it yet.
One honest sentence
Say one true thing to someone you trust that you would normally filter out or soften.
Make something ugly
Create something with no intention of it being good. A sketch, a playlist, a poem. Let perfectionism lose once.
Mirror check-in
Spend 60 seconds with your grounding stone. Hands closed, then open. Notice the difference. Write one word about it.
Checkout

One sentence

Before you leave: one thing you're taking with you from today, and one concrete thing you'll try this week. Specifics help. "I'll try the honest sentence thing with my mom on Thursday" works better than "I'll be more real."
